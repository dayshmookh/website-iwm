Antibiotics in Soil

Antibiotics can be released into the soil system through the excretion of farm animals, application of animal manure on agriculture land as fertilizers or irrigation that utilizes sewage water. These are the main sources whereby antibiotics such as tetracyclines and sulfonamides can get into our environment.

Once antibiotics are released into the soil system, they may persist, bioaccumulate, degrade or be transported to other environment. It is highlighted in the article that sorption, which is the degree to which antibiotics may adsorb to the soil, is an important factor for the bioavailable of antibiotics in soil and this degree is largely influenced by the pH of the soil. Sorption determines the mobility of antibiotics hence, for sulfonamides, which has lesser sorption, is more mobile (Boxall, 2011) and it can move readily from soil to the groundwater or as runoff.

What causes soil Acidity?

Soil acidity is measured by pH level which is the concentration of hydrogen ions in soil. A lower the pH level signifies a more acidic soil and a well maintained pH level will promote the quality of the soil as well as better crop productions.
